Definition

When talking about a functor, one should simultaneously think about the map of objects as well as the map of morphisms. Specifically, the later captures what the functor is really doing. The following properties about a functor basically describe what kind of structures have been preserved by it.

We say a functor \(F\) is faithful if the maps from Mor(A1,A2)Mor(A1,A2) to Mor(F(A1),F(A2))Mor(F(A1),F(A2)) is injective for any A1,A2∈obj(A)A1,A2∈obj(A). Similarly, we say FF is fully if the maps from Mor(A1,A2)Mor(A1,A2) to Mor(F(A1),F(A2))Mor(F(A1),F(A2)) is surjective for any A1,A2∈obj(A)A1,A2∈obj(A). The name “faithfull” and “fully” should make a lot of senses if you think clearly what is going on here. Finally, if the maps from Mor(A1,A2)Mor(A1,A2) to Mor(F(A1),F(A2))Mor(F(A1),F(A2)) is bijective for any A1,A2∈obj(A)A1,A2∈obj(A), then we say FF is fully faithful.

Another common jargon is forgetful whose definition differs from people to people (at least the definition from Brabeeba is different from that from Vakil). Nevertheless, Vakil says a functor FF is forgetfull if some additional structure is forgotten. For example, a functor from ModRModR the module category of ring RR to SetsSets forgets the structure of modules.

An important definitions related to functor is natural transformation, which describes the morphism between functors. But I decided to omit it because it is quite natural so the reader can either imagine how would it be defined or simply go to Vakil or Wikipedia.

Here comes one of the most important concepts in category theory: the universal property. As mentioned in the beginning, universal property provides an unifying method to show the uniqueness of a mathematical object. What does that even mean?

Now, imagine that you want to teach your non-mathematician friend (say your parants) what is a product of two sets AA and BB. You naturally say: “Oh, the product of AA and BB is simply the set {(a,b): a∈A,b∈B}{(a,b): a∈A,b∈B}.” However, imagine that in another parallel universe, you might define the product as {ab:a∈A,b∈B}{ab:a∈A,b∈B}! Is there a way to define the product of AA and BB universally?

Let me try to convince you that the following definition of “the product of AA and BB” would be universal in the sense that the definition in each parallel universe would be isomorphic to each other. The product of sets AA and BB is the set, denoted as say PP, and a pair of morphisms mAmA and mBmB from PP to AA and BB respectively such that the following holds. For any set SS and morphisms m′A,m′BmA′,mB′ from SS to AA and BB respectively, there exists an unique morphism uu from SS to PP such that m′A=mA∘umA′=mA∘u and m′B=mB∘umB′=mB∘u. Technically, people says SS uniquely factors through PP to AA and BB. See the following figure for a pictorial explanation.

As a sanity check, let PP be your definition of product and run through several examples of SS to see if the desired property holds. It is not difficult to see that this in fact can be generalized, for any SS and m′A,m′BmA′,mB′, one can see that u(s)=(m′A(s),m′B(s))u(s)=(mA′(s),mB′(s)) is the unique map from SS to A×BA×B such that SS factors through A×BA×B. Namely, by the universal property, A×BA×B is unique to the definition of product as defined as above up to isomorphism.

Some readers might notice that I’ve been sloppy so far by avoiding defining what exactly universal property is. Having the above concrete example on “product of AA and BB” in mind, now we are ready for a formal definition. Let CC be a category, where you should think of the objects as “SS and its morphisms m′A,m′BmA′,mB′ to AA and BB respectively”. A morphism mm between SS and S′S′ in CC is a mapping such that SS factors through S′S′ through mm. We say an object in CC is an initial object if there is an unique morphism from this object to all the other object. Similarly, we say an object in CC is a final object if there is an unique morphism from all the other object to this object. We say an object is a zero object if it is both initial and final. The following important exercise shows that every initial object (resp. final object) is unique up to isomorphism!

Show that any two initial (resp. final) objects are uniquely isomorhpic with each other.

Now, back to the example of “product of AA and BB”, one can immediately see that PP is a final object from the definition. Namely, it is uniquely defined up to isomorphism (if you did the exercise)!

Some classic examples of universal property are localization and tensor product. However, here I assume the reader might not have the relevant background so I decide to omit these examples. For those who are interested, take a look at Vakil!
