Can you bake the radish pie?

The American League Championship Series of Riddler League Baseball determines one of the teams that will compete in the Riddler World Series. This year’s teams — the Tampa Bay Lines and the Minnesota Twin Primes — are evenly matched. In other words, both teams are equally likely to win each game of the best-of-seven series.

On average, how many games will the series last? (Note that the series ends as soon as one team has won four games.)

This is a simple application of a binomial probability distribution. There are \(2\) ways for the series to end in \(4\) games, there are \(2\cdot{4\choose1}=8\) ways for the series to end in \(5\) games, there are \(2\cdot{5\choose2}=20\) ways for the series to end in \(6\) games, and \(2\cdot{6\choose3}=40\) ways for the series to end in \(7\) games. Thus, our expected value for the number of games is $$2\cdot2^{-4}\cdot 4+8\cdot2^{-5}\cdot5+20\cdot2^{-6}\cdot6+40\cdot2^{-7}\cdot7$$This evaluates to \(\color{red}{5.8125}\) games. The graph above shows a million series simulations and the number of games played. The average number of games played was \(5.811619\).

Riddler Classic

Over in the National League Championship Series, the Washington Rationals and the St. Louis Ordinals (known as the “Ords” for short) are also evenly matched. Again, both teams are equally likely to win each game of the best-of-seven series.

You enter a competition in which you must predict the winner of each of the seven games before the series begins. If any or all of the fifth, sixth or seventh game are not played, you are not credited with predicting a winner.

You win the competition if you predict at least two games correctly. If you optimize your strategy for picking winners, what is the probability you will win the competition?

Extra credit: You enter a second competition in which you must pick the winner of the first game and then of each next game, knowing who won in all the previous games. Again, if you optimize your strategy, now what is the probability you will predict at least two games correctly?

Assume without loss of generality that all strategies must pick Ords for the seventh game (as it makes no difference strategically). We handle each series length separately.

  • Case 4: In this case, one team sweeps the other. So you succeed as long as you have at least \(2\) of your first \(4\) choices as the winning team. If my first \(4\) choices have \(2\) Ords and \(2\) Rationals, I have a \(100\%\) chance of succeeding in this case. Otherwise, I have a \(50\%\) of suceeding.
  • Case 5: By the pigeonhole principle, we assume without loss of generality that we have predicted at least \(3\) ordinal wins. Now for some casework:
    • If we predicted \(5\) ordinal wins, we have a \(50\%\) chance of winning.
    • If we predicted the first \(4\) games would be ordinal wins and the last rational, we have a \(100\%\) chance of winning.
    • If we predicted \(3\) of the first \(4\) games would be ordinal wins, we have a \(87.5\%\) of winning
    • If \(2\) of the first \(4\) games were predicted to be ordinal wins, then we have a \(75\%\) of winning
  • Case 6: Again, without loss of generality, we can assume that Ordinals won exactly \(4/6\) of the games in the series, including the sixth game. If we bet \(2\) or less times on Rationals, we win \(100\%\) of the time. Likewise, if we bet the first \(5\) on Rationals, we win. Now for some casework:
    • If we bet \(3\) out of the first \(5\) were rational wins, the probability of winning is \(1-\frac1{_5C_2}=90\%\).
    • If we bet \(2\) out of the first \(5\) were rational wins and the sixth game went to the rationals, the probability of winning is \(1-\frac{_3C_2}{_5C_2}=70\%\).
    • If we bet \(4\) of the first \(5\) games to be rational wins and the sixth to be an ordinal win, the probability of winning is \(100\%\).
    • If we bet \(4\) of the first \(5\) games to be rational wins and the sixth to be an rational win, the probability of winning is \(1-\frac{_2C_1}{_5C_2}=80\%\)
  • Case 7: Again, we need to do some casework. Each case assumes the previous cases are false.
    • If we bet \(3\) ordinal wins in the first \(6\) games, the probability of winning is \(1-\frac{_3C_3}{_6C_3}=95\%\).
    • If the ordinals win the series, the probability of winning is \(100\%\).
    • If we bet \(0,1,5,6\) games won by the rationals, the probability of winning is \(100\%\).
    • If we bet \(2,4\) games won by the rationals, the probability of winning is \(1-\frac{_3C_2}{_6C_3}=85\%\).

After evaluating all of these possibilities, one finds that a strategy that maximizes the chance of winning, is \(OOOORRR\). In this case, we have a \(\color{red}{90.625\%}\) chance of winning. For the extra credit, observe that the best strategy is to always bet the winning team, since if the winning team loses, the series will go on longer. With this approach, the probability of winning is \(\color{red}{93.75\%}\).
