Can you catch the Cricket?

Help, there’s a cricket on my floor! I want to trap it with a cup so that I can safely move it outside. But every time I get close, it hops exactly 1 foot in a random direction.

I take note of its starting position and come closer. Boom — it hops in a random direction. I get close again. Boom — it takes another hop in a random direction, independent of the direction of the first hop.

What is the most probable distance between the cricket’s current position after two random jumps and its starting position? (Note: This puzzle is not asking for the expected distance, but rather the most probable distance. In other words, if you consider the probability distribution over all possible distances, where is the peak of this distribution?)

Observe that the first jump's direction is irrelevant, since we can just rotate the problem. Thus, we can fix it onto a coordinate grid. Let the starting point of the cricket be at the origin on a coordinate plane, and the location of the cricket after the first jump be \((0,1)\). The possible locations of the cricket after the second jump and the resulting distance from the origin are plotted in the animation above.

The distance from the origin, given an angle \(\theta\) of the new jump from the old jump's direction is given by \(\sqrt{(1+\sin\theta)^2+(\cos\theta)^2}=\sqrt{2+2\sin\theta}\). Note that this function on the domain \(\theta\in(0,2\pi)\) is smooth.

The most probable point is that for which the distance from the origin changes the least with small shifts in \(\theta\). But that is just the derivative! Thus, a point with derivative \(0\) is more probable than a point with non-zero derivative. But there is exactly one point with derivative \(0\): the distance of \(2\) meters.

This may seem counterintuitive: why is the maximum distance the most probable? But it's actually quite simple: the closer to the origin the cricket gets, the faster the distance changes with changes in the angle \(\theta\). So it is to be expected that the most likely point is the farthest one.

Riddler Classic

When you roll a pair of fair dice, the most likely outcome is \(7\) (which occurs \(\frac16\) of the time) and the least likely outcomes are \(2\) and \(12\) (which each occur \(\frac1{36}\) of the time).

Annoyed by the variance of these probabilities, I set out to create a pair of “uniform dice.” These dice still have sides that are uniquely numbered from \(1\) to \(6\), and they are identical to each other. However, they are weighted so that their sum is more uniformly distributed between \(2\) and \(12\) than that of fair dice.

The variance of the \(11\) probabilities is the average value of the squared difference between each probability and the average probability (which is \(\frac1{11}\)). One way to make my dice as uniform as possible is to minimize this variance.

So how should I make my dice as uniform as possible? In other words, which specific weighting of the dice minimizes the variance among the \(11\) probabilities? That is, what should the probabilities be for rolling \(1\), \(2\), \(3\), \(4\), \(5\) or \(6\) with one of the dice?

Let \(p_i\) be the probability of rolling \(i\) on a single die. After some fiddling, I found it was possible to get the variance under \(0.0013\), with $$p_1=p_6=0.25,p_2=p_5=0.16,p_3=p_4=0.09$$I found these values by observing that \(p_1,p_6\) should be the largest as they are involved in the most sums, and \(p_3,p_4\) the smallest by the same token.

I have to confess that I cheated and spent another hour on the problem, and I really have no clue other than grinding it out on my laptop. I guess I'll wait till next week to see how it was done.
