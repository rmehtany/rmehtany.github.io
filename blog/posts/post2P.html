Today, I'm working on the August 13th problems, which were published four days ago

Are you clever enough?

You are very clever when it comes to solving Riddler Express puzzles. You are so clever, in fact, that you are in the top \(10\) percent of solvers in Riddler Nation (which, as you know, has a very large population). You don’t know where in the top \(10\) percent you are — in fact, you realize that you are equally likely to be anywhere in the topmost decile. Also, no two people in Riddler Nation are equally clever.

One Friday morning, you walk into a room with nine members randomly selected from Riddler Nation. What is the probability that you are the cleverest solver in the room?

Suppose that the probability of being smarter than a randomly chosen individual is \(p\). Clearly, based on the given information, \(p\) ranges between \(0.9\) and \(1\). I'm assuming uniformity since we aren't provided any additional information, so it is equally likely that \(p\) is at any point in \([0.9,1]\).

The probability of us being the smartest in the room in a randomly chosen room of \(10\) people given \(p\) is just \(p^9\) by the independence assumption of the randomly chosen room. So, our answer is just $$\int\limits_{0.9}^1p^9\:dp\sim0.651$$

Riddler Classic

You have four standard dice, and your goal is simple: Maximize the sum of your rolls. So you roll all four dice at once, hoping to achieve a high score. But wait, there’s more! If you’re not happy with your roll, you can choose to reroll zero, one, two or three of the dice. In other words, you must “freeze” one or more dice and set them aside, never to be rerolled.

You repeat this process with the remaining dice — you roll them all and then freeze at least one. You repeat this process until all the dice are frozen. If you play strategically, what score can you expect to achieve on average?

I couldn't find a nice approach to this, so I just considered two dice to start. If they both come up above \(4\), freeze both and call it a day. This has an expected value of \(10\). Otherwise, freeze the larger die and roll the other again. If one of the dice rolls is above \(3\), then the expected value is \(5+3.5=8.5\). If both dice rolls are below \(4\), casework gets an expected value of \(\frac{107}{18}\).

So the overall expected value of the game is \(\frac{593}{72}\). I did a similar process for \(3\) dice (which involved some pretty annoying case work) to get \(13.42\), but then ran out of time. I'm pretty confident with more time, I would have been able to finish the problem, but it certainly was a challenging one!

Conclusion
