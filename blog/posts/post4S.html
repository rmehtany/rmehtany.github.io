Right before the start of the 2020 Australian Open, the tennis world was shaken by two cases of temporary disqualification for doping. The first starred the Chilean Nicolas Jarry, who was suspended from playing after a urine check that contained metabolic traces of ligandrol and stanozolol, two prohibited anabolic substances.

The second player who was suspended in a similar fashion was Colombian Robert Farah, who is a Grand Slam champion in men’s doubles with fellow Colombian Juan Sebastian Cabal. Farah and Cabal are also the no. 1 men’s doubles team in the world.

Farah is guilty of having ingested "Boldenona", a supplement sold in Colombia as a veterinary prescription, widely used in beef breeding. Both players released statements reiterating they were innocent and promised to demonstrate their estrangement from such uses at the appropriate venues.

While we don't know whether these accusations have any merit, they are shocking as the concept of doping is so rarely brought up in tennis. in my view, that has far more to do with the lax approach the ITF has taken towards doping, rather than any sort of cleanliness on part of the athletes.

Let's be very clear: when we talk about "sports medicine" improving the career length of players like the Big 3, we aren't just talking about massages. Doping is a huge part of the reason the Big 3, among others, have been able to dominate this era in a way other legends haven't dominated their eras. When you think about Federer's 2017 "renaissance", or Djokovic's 2011 transformation, or Nadal's ability to maintain his physique while recovering ridiculously quickly, all most likely have something to do with EPO, the drug most commonly found in endurance sports like cycling to boost recovery time for athletes. Interestingly enough, tennis doesn't really test for EPO, at least not consistently. In fact, they don't test for a lot of the major steroid and performance enhancing drugs littering other sports.

What tennis does test for is recreational drug use. Hingis got busted for cocaine. Cilic for consuming a glucose tablet. Only when the ITF wants to show they care about doping, they'll pick a fall man: someone who's blatantly flaunting the rules, like Sharapova. Don't think that her getting busted has anything to do with better drug testing as part of the ITF's new committee.

Why doesn't the ITF do better? Currently, despite tennis being one of the most watched and profitable sports on the planet, it drug tests on the level of sports far smaller than it, like handball and pickleball, rather than sports like cycling which do a much more comprehensive job with testing. But therein lies the catch-22. If more tennis players are caught using PEDs (which is what would happen for sure if testing got better: several companies have admitted to selling to top 100 players), that would make the public think the sport is less clean, despite the opposite being true. It's why tennis turns a blind eye to the case.

But this makes tennis an even more unequal sport. Top players, who have the best doctors on call to concoct whatever formula they need to recover and play their best, will never be touched by players who can only afford basic PEDs. It's not surprising at all that these top players are whooping everyone, in my mind.

Of course, this doesn't invalidate the Big 3 (or any players) accomplishments. Performance boosters don't work unless you have the drive and commitment to push yourself to the best version of yourself. But it does qualify the success of the Big 3, and really tennis as a whole. It's rigged towards those who have the resources to cheat smarter. That means big tennis federations, teen prodigies, and wealthy parents are all too important in reaching the pinnacle of the sport.
