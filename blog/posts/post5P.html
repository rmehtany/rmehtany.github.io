Can you bake the radish pie?

I recently came across a rather peculiar recipe for something called Babylonian radish pie. Intrigued, I began to follow the directions, which said I could start with any number of cups of flour.

Any number? I mean, I had to start with some flour, so zero cups wasn’t an option. But according to the recipe, any positive value was fair game. Next, I needed a second amount of flour that was \(3\) divided by my original number. For example, if I had started with two cups of flour, then the recipe told me I now needed \(3\) divided by \(2\), or \(1.5\), cups at this point.

I was then instructed to combine these amounts of flour and discard half. Apparently, this was my new starting amount of flour. I was to repeat the process, combining this amount with \(3\) divided by it and then discarding half.

The recipe told me to keep doing this, over and over. Eventually, I’d have the proper number of cups of flour for my radish pie. How many cups of flour does the recipe ultimately call for?

This is a classic example of an attractive fixed point. Let's consider cases on \(c_k\), the number of cups of flour at time \(k\).

Case 1: \(c_k=\sqrt3\). In this case, we have $$\frac12\left(c_k+\frac3{c_k}\right)=\frac12\left(\sqrt3+\frac3{\sqrt3}\right)=\sqrt3$$This is called a fixed point, as applying an iteration doesn't affect the total number of cups of flour.

Case 2: . Note that the function \(f(x)=\frac12\left(x+\frac3x\right)\) is differentiable on \((0,\infty\), it has derivative \(1-\frac3{x^2}\) and second derivative \(\frac6{x^3}\). Thus, it has positive second derivative on \((0,\infty)\), meaning that it has a global minimum, and that point must appear at a point with derivative \(0\). There is only one such point in \((0,\infty)\), which is \(\sqrt3\). Thus, the output of any iteration of this process will always end up in either Case 1 or Case 3. So we can ignore this case.

Case 3: \(c_k>\sqrt3\). In this case, we know that \(c_{k+1}\) will also be in case 1 or case 3 from the argument in case 2. So, let's compute the ratio of the differences \(c_{k+1}-\sqrt3\) and \(c_k-\sqrt3\). We have $$c_{k+1}-\sqrt3=\frac{c_k}2+\frac3{2c_k}-\sqrt3$$$$=\frac{c_k^2-2\sqrt3c_k+3}{2c_k}$$$$=\frac{(c_k-\sqrt3)^2}{2c_k}$$Thus, $$0<\frac{c_{k+1}-\sqrt3}{c_k-\sqrt3}=\frac{c_k-\sqrt3}{2c_k}<\frac12$$This tells us that each iteration gets us at least twice as close to \(\sqrt3\) as the previous iteration, which necessarily implies that we will converge to \(\sqrt3\).
