Riddler Express

I have a spherical pumpkin. I carefully calculate its volume in cubic inches, as well as its surface area in square inches. Next, I got up to have a piece of Halloween candy.

But when I came back to my calculations, I saw that my units — the square inches and the cubic inches — had mysteriously disappeared from my calculations. But it didn’t matter, because both numerical values were the same!

What is the radius of my spherical pumpkin?

Extra credit: Let’s dispense with 3D thinking. Instead, suppose I have an \(n\)-hyperspherical pumpkin. Once again, I calculate its volume (with units in\(^n\)) and surface area (with units in\(^{n−1}\)). Miraculously, the numerical values are once again the same! What is the radius of my \(n\)-hyperspherical pumpkin?

Let \(S\) be the surface area of the \(n\)-hypersphere with radius \(1\). We know that the surface area of the \(n\) hypersphere with radius \(r\) is \(Sr^{n-1}\). Thus, the volume of a \(n\)-hypersphere with radius \(r\) is $$V(r)=\int\limits_0^rSx^{n-1}dx=\frac Snr^n$$Let \(r\) be the radius for which \(Sr^{n-1}=V(r)\). So, we have $$\frac Snr^n=Sr^{n-1}$$So, \(\frac rn=1\), and \(r=n\). So, if the magnitude of the radius is the dimension, the magnitudes of the surface area and volume are the same. Thus, the answer is \(r=3\).

The Squiddler

Congratulations, you’ve made it to the fifth round of The Squiddler — a competition that takes place on a remote island. In this round, you are one of the \(16\) remaining competitors who must cross a bridge made up of \(18\) pairs of separated glass squares. The diagram above shows an aerial view of the game.

To cross the bridge, you must jump from one pair of squares to the next. However, you must choose one of the two squares in a pair to land on. Within each pair, one square is made of tempered glass, while the other is made of normal glass. If you jump onto tempered glass, all is well, and you can continue on to the next pair of squares. But if you jump onto normal glass, it will break, and you will be eliminated from the competition.

You and your competitors have no knowledge of which square within each pair is made of tempered glass. The only way to figure it out is to take a leap of faith and jump onto a square. Once a pair is revealed — either when someone lands on a tempered square or a normal square — all remaining competitors take notice and will choose the tempered glass when they arrive at that pair.

On average, how many of the \(16\) competitors will survive and make it to the next round of the competition?

Let's rate the players from \(1\to16\), depending on how risk-taking they are. If \(1\) is alive, they will always jump first onto the next pair before anyone else does. If \(1\) is dead, \(2\) will jump first, etc. This obviously has no effect on the game, but simplifies our calculations.

Let \(k\) be the lowest remaining player (i.e., the risk-taker) at pair \(j\). Thus, there are \(16-k+1\) players remaining before the jump to pair \(j+1\). At pair \(j+1\), there is a \(0.5\) chance of losing player \(k\), leaving us with \(16-k\) players. There is a \(0.5\) chance of not losing any players, leaving us with \(16-k+1\) players. So, on average, we lose \(0.5\) players each pair, regardless of how many players are left. So, each pair can be viewed as an independent event.

With this knowledge, the problem is simple. The expected value of the the number of players lost is the sum of the expected values of the number of players lost at each pair. This is just \(18\cdot0.5=9\). So, we expect to lose \(9\) players, and thus we expect to have \(7\) players survive.
