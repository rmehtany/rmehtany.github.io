One of the less pleasant parts of being home in these pandemic times has been listening to my younger sister gripe about her high school friendships, and all of the social faux pas she's made. The fact that this even makes my list of the worst parts of quarantine should tell you quite a lot about my quarantine experience. Yes, I've had several valued relatives pass too soon, and yes, I have (like most) felt the mental burden caused by the lockdowns, but when all is said and done, I've been extraordinarily lucky to have a comfortable home and stay-at-home job to keep me going during the pandemic.

I don't think I've ever had an issue with acknowledging my fortunate circumstances. But there was a time when I was uncomfortable with sharing that awareness with others. I used to think that talking about my luck was at best lowering the value of my hard work, and just plain condescending. No one wants to hear an eight minute Macklemore song about white privilege.

That discomfort was multiplied by my internal comparisons to my father. While I grew up in a typical American middle class household, he grew up in a village without electricity, flowing water, or even a full school system. Despite that, he has "succeeded" in life, both in terms of his career, as well as his happiness. How can I compare my achievements to that? It's impossible.

I was reminded of all of this thanks to the video Is success luck or hard work? by Derek Muller. (Fun fact: I've actually met Derek a few years ago. I'll talk about that some other time.)
