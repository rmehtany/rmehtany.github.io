One of my favorite blogs to visit when I have free time is eyleMMath as it has a lot of pretty visualizations of geometric concepts.

I was fascinated by a few polygon diagrams I saw on the site, so I'll try to demonstrate why the diagrams are true. Every polygon you see in this post is a regular polygon with side length \(2\).

Adding squares of partitions of the main diagonal

The picture above shows a rather peculiar summation of the partitions of the main diagonal of regular polygons. In fact, for a \(n\)-gon with side length \(2\), the sum of squares of these partitions is \(n\).

This curious relation is actually quite difficult to prove. Let's start with the following diagram of a regular heptagon and octagon.

Note that angle \(c_4\) extends out of the octagon. This is because \(c_4\) is defined to be the supplementary angle to \(d_4\). This will be necessary for any even sided polygon.

Note that since for all \(b_i\), \(\sin b_i=\sin a_i\), and similarly for \(c_i,d_i\), observe that \(2\sin a_i=x_i\) for all \(i\), and likewise, \(2\sin c_i=y_i\) for all \(i\).

Note that \(a_1=\pi/7\) since the interior angle of the heptagon is \(pi-\frac{2\pi}7\), and \(a_1\) is the complement of half of the interior angle. More generally, for a \(n\) sided polygon, \(a_1=\pi/n\).

And, for any \(a_i,a_{i+1}\), \(a_i+(\pi-a_{i+1})=a_i+b_i=\pi-\frac{2\pi}7\), so \(a_{i+1}-a_i=\frac{2\pi}7\). More generally, for a \(n\) sided polygon, \(a_{i+1}-a_i=\frac{2\pi}n\).

Thus, \(a_i\) (and similarly, \(c_i\)) forms an arithmetic progression. In particular, we can write$$x_1^2+x_2^2+x_3^2=4\sin^2\left(\frac\pi7\right)+4\sin^2\left(\frac{3\pi}7\right)+4\sin^2\left(\frac{5\pi}7\right)$$A similar formula holds for the octagon. More generally, we can write the sum of the squares of the lengths of the partitions as$$4\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor}\sin^2\left((2k-1)\frac\pi n\right)$$

Via the relation \(\sin^2\theta=\frac12\cdot(1-\cos2\theta)\), we can rewrite as$$2\cdot\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor-2\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor}\cos\left((2k-1)\cdot\frac{2\pi}n\right)$$

Remembering that \(\cos\theta=\mathfrak{Re}(e^{i\theta})\) and that the real part of a sum of complex numbers is the sum of the real parts of those numbers, we have $$2\cdot\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor-2\mathfrak{Re}\left(\sum\limits_{k=0}^{\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor-1}\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)\cdot\left(\exp\left(\frac{4i\pi}n\right)\right)^k\right)$$We can evaluate the geometric series to get $$2\cdot\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor-2\mathfrak{Re}\left(\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)\cdot\frac{1-\exp\left(\frac{4i\pi}n\right)^{\left\lfloor\frac n2\right\rfloor}}{1-\exp\left(\frac{4i\pi}n\right)}\right)$$If \(n=2m\) for some integer \(m>1\), then this becomes$$n-2\mathfrak{Re}\left(\frac{\exp\left(\frac{i\pi}m\right)-\exp\left(\frac{i\pi}m\right)^{n+1}}{1-\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}m\right)}\right)$$However,$$\exp\left(\frac{i\pi}m\right)^{n+1}=\exp\left(\frac{i\pi}m\right)\exp(2i\pi)=\exp\left(\frac{i\pi}m\right)$$implies that the numerator of the fraction is \(0\), and thus, the expression is \(n\). If \(n=2m+1\) for some positive integer \(m\), we have $$n-1-2\mathfrak{Re}\left(\frac{\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)-\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)^n}{1-\exp\left(\frac{4i\pi}n\right)}\right)$$$$n-1-2\mathfrak{Re}\left(\frac{\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)-1}{1-\exp\left(\frac{4i\pi}n\right)}\right)$$$$n-1+2\mathfrak{Re}\left(\frac1{1+\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)}\right)$$If we let the expression inside the large parentheses be \(w\), observe that since complex conjugation respects both multiplication and addition, \(\overline w=\frac1{1+\exp\left(\frac{-2i\pi}n\right)}\).

Thus, $$w+\overline w=\frac1{1+\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)}+\frac{\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)}{\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)}\cdot\frac1{1+\exp\left(\frac{-2i\pi}n\right)}=\frac1{1+\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)}+\frac{\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)}{1+\exp\left(\frac{2i\pi}n\right)}=1$$So, \(\mathfrak{Re}(w)=0.5\), and so the expression above is \(n\).

That concludes the proof of the diagram! To summarize, given any regular polygon with side length \(2\), the sum of the squares of the lengths of the partitions of the main diagonal sum to the number of sides in the polygon. That's neat!
