After the last Project Euler post, I wanted to see if I could be one of the first 100 solvers of a Project Euler Problem. So when Problem 770 was released on Halloween, I couldn't help but rush to be one of the first solvers. Luckily for me, I ended up being one of the first 25 solvers of the problem! Let's discuss the ideas behind Problem 770 and another recent Project Euler problem. As always, I won't fully solve the problem, so that all of you can enjoy the process of solving it too.

Delphi Flip

A and B play a game. A has originally \(1\) gram of gold and B has an unlimited amount. Each round goes as follows:

1. A chooses and displays, \(x\), a nonnegative real number no larger than the amount of gold that A has.

2. Either B chooses to TAKE. Then A gives B \(x\) grams of gold. Or B chooses to GIVE. Then B gives A \(x\) grams of gold.

3. B TAKEs \(n\) times and GIVEs \(n\) times after which the game finishes.

Define \(g(X)\) to be the smallest value of \(n\) so that A can guarantee to have at least \(X\) grams of gold at the end of the game. You are given \(g(1.7) = 10\).

Find \(g(1.9999)\).

Let \(h(a,b)\) be the maximum amount of gold A can guarantee in a game with \(b\) TAKEs and \(a\) GIVEs. Thus, the problem can be rephrased as finding the smallest \(n\) for which \(h(n,n)>X\).

Observe that \(h(0,b)=1\), since A will just set \(x=0\) in each round and lose nothing. Likewise, \(h(a,0)=2^a\), since A will bet all of its gold each round to double it.

Now how do we compute \(h(a,b)\) in general? Assuming that \(a,b\neq0\), consider the first round. Note that the smaller \(x\) is, the larger \(h(a,b)\) is assuming that B TAKEs on the first round. Likewise, the larger \(x\) is, the larger \(h(a,b)\) is assuming that B GIVEs on the first round.

Thus, to maximize the gold received, A must ensure that the gold output that is received would be the same regardless of whether B TAKEs or GIVEs. In other words, $$(1+x)\cdot h(a-1,b)=(1-x)\cdot h(a,b-1)$$Rearranging, we have $$x=\frac{h(a,b-1)-h(a-1,b)}{h(a,b-1)+h(a-1,b)}$$Plugging that into the first equation, we have $$h(a,b)=(1+x)\cdot h(a-1,b)=\frac{2h(a-1,b)h(a,b-1)}{h(a-1,b)+h(a,b-1)}$$This gives us a recursion that theoretically solves our problem.

Using this recursion, I've plotted a graph of \(n\) by \(h(n,n)\) above. As you can see, \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}h(n,n)=2\). However, if you try to compute \(h(n,n)\) for large values of \(n\) this way, you won't be able to solve the problem, even on a very fast computer. My computer took around 2 hours to compute \(h(10000,10000)\sim1.9974\), even with optimizations that sacrificed accuracy, not to mention around 50 MB of storage. So a recursive approach won't work.

Instead, we have to find a closed form for \(h(n,n)\). I won't spoil how to do this, as this is the meat of the problem, but I'll provide the following hint: Can you find some function \(g(a,b)=f(h(a,b))\) such that expressing \(g(a,b)\) in terms of \(g(a-1,b)\) and \(g(a,b-1)\) is much simpler than the recursion above? Will that help you find a general form?

Concatenation Coincidence

A non-decreasing sequence of integers \(a_n\) can be generated from any positive real value \(\theta\) by the following procedure: \begin{align} \begin{split} b_1 &= \theta \\ b_n &= \left\lfloor b_{n-1} \right\rfloor \left(b_{n-1} - \left\lfloor b_{n-1} \right\rfloor + 1\right)~~~\forall ~ n \geq 2 \\ a_n &= \left\lfloor b_{n} \right\rfloor \end{split} \end{align} Where \(\left\lfloor . \right\rfloor\) is the floor function.

For example, \(\theta=2.956938891377988...\) generates the Fibonacci sequence: \(2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, 55, 89, ...\)

The concatenation of a sequence of positive integers \(a_n\) is a real value denoted \(\tau\) constructed by concatenating the elements of the sequence after the decimal point, starting at \(a_1\): \(a_1.a_2a_3a_4...\)

For example, the Fibonacci sequence constructed from \(\theta=2.956938891377988...\) yields the concatenation $$\tau=2.3581321345589...$$Clearly, \(\tau \neq \theta\) for this value of \(\theta\).

Find the only value of \(\theta\) for which the generated sequence starts at \(a_1=2\) and the concatenation of the generated sequence equals the original value: \(\tau = \theta\). Give your answer rounded to 24 places after the decimal point.

In my opinion, this problem is rather trivial to solve (each digit is determined by the previous, a simple Python script will give you the answer). Rather, I'm more interested in the generalization, dealing with different values of \(a_1\).

First and foremost, not every choice of \(a_1\) leads to a solution. For example, \(a_1=3\) has no solutions (if you've solved the problem, the same script can prove this to be true with a slight modification).

\(a_1=1\) unsurprisingly leads to the solution \(1.1111\ldots\). \(a_1=4\) has two solutions, as if we directly apply the solution to \(a_1=2\) here, we'll get stuck at \(a_3\) as there are two possibilities for it, both of which lead to valid solutions.

I've been playing around with these solutions for a few hours now, and I still haven't found any pattern governing the number of solutions based on \(a_1=5\). If somebody knows a better way to understand these solutions, feel free to enlighten me in the comments.
